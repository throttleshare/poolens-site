CYA Pool Stabilizer: The Complete Guide for Techs

August 25, 2025 Chemistry 9 min read

Cyanuric acid (CYA) might be the most misunderstood chemical in pool service. Done right, it stretches chlorine dollars and protects pools from UV burn-off. Done wrong — or ignored — it turns into the silent killer that makes a pool look balanced on paper while chlorine does almost nothing in practice. This guide covers everything a working tech needs to know.

What CYA Actually Does

Free chlorine (hypochlorous acid, HOCl) is destroyed by UV light rapidly. In direct midday sun, an unstabilized outdoor pool can lose 50–90% of its free chlorine within two hours. Cyanuric acid works as a UV shield by temporarily bonding with hypochlorous acid to form chlorinated isocyanurates — compounds that are protected from photodegradation but can release active chlorine as the pool consumes it.

Think of CYA as a reservoir that buffers chlorine against UV destruction. Without it, every outdoor pool would require constant dosing during the day to maintain any residual. With it, one morning dose of chlorine can last through the afternoon.

Key concept — chlorine-CYA equilibrium: CYA doesn't destroy chlorine. It holds a fraction of it in a temporarily inactive form. The higher your CYA, the larger the fraction held inactive. At 30 ppm CYA, roughly 3% of chlorine is bound. At 90 ppm CYA, roughly 9% is bound. The problem is that those percentages compound against efficacy faster than the numbers suggest.

How to Add CYA

Granular Cyanuric Acid (Stabilizer)

Granular CYA is the most common form. It dissolves slowly and must be handled carefully to avoid clogging returns or settling in one spot.

  1. Calculate the dose needed (see table below)
  2. Place granules in a mesh sock or skimmer basket sock
  3. Hang in skimmer or hold near a return jet to circulate
  4. Allow pump to run continuously for 24–48 hours
  5. Do not backwash for at least 48 hours — CYA will wash out
  6. Retest after 48 hours before adding more
Warning — CYA test lag: CYA levels appear lower than actual until fully dissolved. Adding more because your test reads low while granules are still dissolving is a common way to overshoot. Always wait the full 48 hours before retesting and re-dosing.

Liquid Cyanuric Acid

Liquid stabilizer (usually 50% concentration) acts immediately and won't clog equipment. Pour slowly into skimmer or around the perimeter with the pump running. More expensive per pound of CYA, but preferred for precise small adjustments.

Trichlor Tablets and Dichlor

Both trichlor tablets and dichlor granules contain cyanuric acid bound to the chlorine molecule. Every time you add chlorine via these sources, you're also adding CYA. Trichlor is approximately 52–57% CYA by weight. The relationship:

The CYA-Chlorine Relationship: Why High CYA Is Dangerous

This is where most pool owners get confused — and where good techs earn their pay. CYA doesn't reduce the reading of free chlorine on a test kit. It reduces its effectiveness. A pool can show 3.0 ppm free chlorine and still be functionally unable to sanitize properly if CYA is at 150 ppm.

The key concept is minimum effective free chlorine (FC) by CYA level. The pool industry uses the "7.5% rule" as a rough guide — for basic sanitation, FC should be at least 7.5% of CYA. For algae prevention, 10–15% is safer.

The 90 ppm hard ceiling: The APSP and most state health codes cap CYA at 100 ppm in commercial pools, and best practice is to never let residential pools exceed 90 ppm. Above that threshold, you'd need to maintain 7–10 ppm of free chlorine just to meet minimum sanitation — which is both impractical and damaging to vinyl liners and equipment.

Testing CYA Accurately

CYA is measured via turbidimetric (cloudiness) comparison. The standard method uses a black dot turbidity tube — you add reagent to the sample and look down the tube until the dot disappears, reading the level at the side scale.

  • Taylor K-2006 kit: The professional gold standard. Reagent R-0013 (melamine) is highly accurate
  • LaMotte ColorQ: Digital photometric reading, eliminates the subjective dot comparison
  • Strips: Moderately accurate in the 30–100 ppm range. Fine for quick checks between full tests
  • DPD test kits: Most standard DPD kits do NOT test CYA. Use the specific turbidity test

For commercial pools or disputed readings, send a sample to a certified lab. The turbidity method can be confused by high turbidity from algae or other particles — lab analysis uses HPLC for accurate results.

How to Lower CYA

There is no chemical method for reducing CYA that works reliably in a pool environment. Marketing claims aside, the only proven approaches are:

  • Partial drain and refill: Drain 25–50% of pool volume, refill with fresh water, rebalance. Each 25% drain lowers CYA by roughly 25%.
  • Reverse osmosis filtration: A mobile RO unit can reduce CYA from 150 to 30 ppm without draining, while preserving salts, calcium, and other minerals. Costs $400–800 per treatment but saves significant water.
  • Switch chlorine sources: Stop using trichlor and switch to liquid chlorine or cal-hypo. CYA will slowly drop as water evaporates and is topped off with fresh water.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the ideal CYA level for a pool?

For outdoor chlorine pools: 30–50 ppm. Salt chlorine generator pools: 60–80 ppm (higher CYA needed because SWG output varies). Above 90 ppm, chlorine efficacy drops significantly regardless of free chlorine reading.

How long does it take for CYA to dissolve in a pool?

Granular stabilizer added in a skimmer sock typically dissolves in 24–48 hours with the pump running. Liquid cyanuric acid is immediately available. Do not backwash for at least 48 hours after adding granular CYA.

Can you lower CYA without draining the pool?

No practical chemical method exists to reduce CYA in pool water. The only reliable solutions are partial drain-and-refill or reverse osmosis filtration. There is no "CYA reducer" chemical that works reliably at pool scale.

Does cyanuric acid affect pH or alkalinity?

Cyanuric acid is a weak acid. At typical doses, it has minimal impact on pH. However at very high CYA levels (100+ ppm), it can contribute to a slight pH drop and throws off standard alkalinity test kits.

Why does trichlor add so much CYA?

Trichlor tablets are 52–57% cyanuric acid by weight. Every 10 ppm of chlorine added via trichlor also adds approximately 6 ppm of CYA. A pool relying entirely on tablets can gain 30–60 ppm of CYA per season without any additional stabilizer.
