Going Independent vs Joining a Pool Franchise: Real Talk

📅 April 14, 2026Pool Pro⏱ 6 min read

The pool service franchise question comes up constantly in forums and industry groups, and the answers range from "franchises are a scam" to "I couldn't have built my business without the support." Both extremes exist because the right answer genuinely depends on your situation. Here is a clear-eyed comparison that doesn't favor either path — just the factors that actually matter for your decision.

What Franchises Actually Offer

Before deciding whether a franchise is worth the cost, you need to understand specifically what you are paying for. Legitimate pool service franchises provide some combination of:

  • Training program — typically 1–2 weeks of classroom and field training on chemistry, equipment, and business operations. Value: high for someone entering from outside the industry; low for experienced technicians.
  • Brand recognition — in markets where the franchise has strong presence, this can accelerate client acquisition. In markets where the brand is unknown, it provides no advantage over a well-named independent.
  • Chemical supplier pricing — franchises negotiate bulk pricing with chemical distributors that individual operators can't match. This is one of the most concrete ongoing benefits. Savings of 10–20% on chemical costs are realistic.
  • Business management systems — scheduling software, invoicing, client communication templates. Value depends on whether you would build or buy these independently.
  • Lead generation — some franchises funnel central marketing inquiries to franchisees. This is highly variable — some franchises deliver; others don't.
  • Exit value — franchise affiliation may increase the sale price of your route if the brand is strong enough that buyers pay a premium. This is market-specific.

The Real Cost of a Franchise

The franchise fee and royalty structure deserves careful analysis before signing.

Cost ComponentTypical RangeNotes
Initial franchise fee$30,000–$70,000Paid upfront; non-refundable
Royalty fee6–10% of gross revenuePaid monthly, forever
Marketing/advertising fee1–3% of gross revenuePaid to franchise fund, not your local marketing
Required purchasesVariesSome franchises require buying chemicals/equipment from approved suppliers

On a $200,000/year gross revenue route, a 7% royalty is $14,000/year. Over 10 years, that is $140,000 — plus the initial fee. The question is whether the franchise delivers more than $140,000 in value over that period through chemical discounts, leads, and operational support. For some operators in the right markets, it does. For others, it definitively does not.

Read the Franchise Disclosure Document (FDD) before signing anything. The FDD includes audited financial statements from the franchisor, a list of all current and former franchisees (call them), litigation history, and the specific terms of every fee. You are legally entitled to receive this document and to review it with an attorney before committing. Never sign a franchise agreement without having an attorney who specializes in franchise law review it.

The Case for Independent

The pool service industry is uniquely suited to independent operation because:

  • Clients choose their pool company primarily on reputation and referrals, not brand recognition. A local company with an excellent reputation in your neighborhood beats a national franchise brand every time.
  • Training resources are freely available. NSPF online courses, PHTA's CPO program, manufacturer training portals, and Trouble Free Pool's online chemistry school give you everything a franchise training program offers without the fee.
  • Chemical buying groups for independent operators exist. GENESIS 3, Pool & Hot Tub Alliance membership, and regional buying cooperatives give independent operators access to better pricing than franchise mandated suppliers in many cases.
  • Your brand, your rules. You can specialize, price your own way, hire who you want, and build an asset that reflects your values. A franchise agreement restricts many of these choices contractually.

When a Franchise Makes Sense

There are specific situations where franchise affiliation genuinely makes sense:

  • You are entering from a completely unrelated field and have no industry contacts, no training, and no idea where to start. The franchise training program and initial support structure accelerates your first year.
  • The franchise has documented strong brand presence in your specific market, verified by talking to existing franchisees about their actual lead volume from the brand.
  • The franchise has a specific technology platform or chemical supply pricing that you independently verified delivers savings beyond the royalty cost.
  • You plan to build a multi-tech operation quickly and want a proven operational system rather than building your own management infrastructure.

The single best research step before deciding on any franchise: call five current franchisees and five former franchisees (both lists are in the FDD). Ask former franchisees specifically why they left. The patterns in those calls will tell you more than any marketing material the franchisor provides.

The Middle Path: Buying an Independent Route

For most people entering pool service with some capital, buying an independent route from a retiring operator is often the best option: immediate cash flow, established client relationships, an existing reputation, and no ongoing royalty obligations. Route brokers in most states list available residential routes regularly. This path skips the slow organic growth phase without incurring franchise overhead.

Frequently Asked Questions

What are the biggest pool service franchises?

The largest pool service franchises in the United States include Poolwerx and several regional franchise groups. No single national franchise dominates the residential pool service industry the way some other service franchises do — the market remains highly fragmented and local, which is an advantage for independent operators.

How much does a pool service franchise cost?

Pool service franchise initial fees typically range from $30,000–$70,000, plus royalty fees of 6–10% of gross revenue ongoing. Total initial investment (fee + working capital + equipment + vehicle) often runs $80,000–$150,000.

Can I buy a pool route without a franchise?

Yes. The majority of pool service routes available for sale are independent operations — no franchise affiliation required. Route brokers facilitate these transactions. Routes are typically priced at 10–15 times monthly gross revenue for residential accounts. An independent purchase keeps 100% of your gross with no ongoing royalties.

What do pool franchises actually provide?

Legitimate pool franchises typically provide: initial training program, brand recognition, preferred chemical supplier pricing, business management software, an established service protocol, and ongoing operational support. The value of each depends heavily on the specific franchise and whether you need what they offer.
