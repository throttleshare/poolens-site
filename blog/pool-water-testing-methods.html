Pool Water Testing: Strip vs Liquid vs Digital vs Pro Lab

September 14, 2025 Chemistry 9 min read

Every pool chemical decision you make is only as good as the test it is based on. A pool that tests at 3.0 ppm FC with a faded strip kit and an accurate 2.1 ppm FC with a Taylor K-2006 leads to two completely different service decisions — one of which leaves the pool under-chlorinated. Understanding the accuracy limitations and appropriate use cases of each testing method is foundational professional knowledge.

Test Strips: Quick But Limited

Test strips use pad-based reagents that change color in response to chemical concentrations. The user matches the color to a reference card. Major strip brands include Aquachek, HTH, Poolmaster, and Hach.

When Strips Work Adequately

  • Homeowner daily FC and pH check between weekly service visits
  • Quick screening before deciding whether full testing is needed
  • Parameters within the middle of the normal range (FC 1–5 ppm, pH 7.0–8.0)

Strip Limitations

  • Wash-out at high FC: Most strips max out at 5–8 ppm. At SLAM levels (12–30 ppm), strips read zero — completely misleading during treatment
  • No combined chlorine measurement: Strips measure total or free chlorine only — cannot separate CC from FC
  • Color vision sensitivity: The human eye interprets color differently under different lighting (fluorescent vs sunlight vs shadow). Two different techs can read the same strip and get different results
  • Expiration dependency: Strips degrade after opening the tube. Six-month-old strips in a humid shed may read significantly off even before expiration date
Strips are not appropriate for SLAM management. At SLAM FC levels, test strip chlorine pads wash out and read zero or near-zero even when FC is 20+ ppm. A technician managing a green pool recovery based on strip readings will massively over-dose or under-dose because the test gives no usable data at treatment concentrations. Use FAS-DPD only during SLAM.

DPD Liquid Drop Tests

DPD (N,N-diethyl-p-phenylenediamine) colorimetric tests use liquid reagents added to a measured water sample in a comparator block. Color is compared to a reference standard. The Taylor K-1000 series and similar kits use this method.

Standard DPD tests can measure FC and CC separately (critical for identifying chloramine problems), pH with better repeatability than strips, and total alkalinity via titration. They are significantly more accurate than strips and appropriate for most routine service testing.

DPD limitation: Like strips, DPD test reagents "wash out" at high free chlorine levels. At above 10 ppm FC, standard DPD turns colorless — giving a false zero reading. At 15–20 ppm, the test is completely invalid.

FAS-DPD Drop Tests: The Professional Standard

FAS-DPD (ferrous ammonium sulfate - DPD) is a titration-based method that overcomes the DPD wash-out limitation. The Taylor K-2006 Complete Kit is the benchmark professional test kit and uses FAS-DPD for chlorine measurement.

Why FAS-DPD Is Superior

  • Accurate through the full SLAM range and beyond — no wash-out at any practical FC level
  • Titration method eliminates the subjective color comparison — you count drops until color changes, not interpret a color gradient
  • Measures FC and CC separately with precision
  • Same kit includes TA, CH, pH, and CYA tests with matching accuracy
Taylor K-2006 vs K-2005: The K-2006 includes FAS-DPD for chlorine measurement (more accurate, works at all levels). The K-2005 uses standard DPD (washes out at high chlorine). The K-2006 is the professional standard — the K-2005 is for homeowner use. The price difference is worth it.

Digital Photometric Testers

Digital photometric readers (LaMotte ColorQ Pro 9, Hach Pocket Colorimeter, Pentair Electronic Test Kit) use LED light sources and sensors to measure color change with less subjective error than human vision. They provide numerical readouts rather than requiring color comparison.

Advantages

  • Eliminates color-vision variability between technicians
  • Faster than drop titration for most tests
  • Easy to train staff — results are numerical, not interpretive
  • Many models automatically calculate chemical dose recommendations based on pool volume and readings

Limitations

  • Initial cost ($150–400) is higher than drop test kits
  • Cell/cuvette must be kept scrupulously clean — contamination causes errors
  • Batteries and electronics fail in field conditions; a backup drop kit is advisable
  • Not all meters use FAS-DPD for chlorine — check before purchasing if SLAM accuracy matters

Certified Lab Analysis

Professional water analysis labs provide comprehensive testing including metals (iron, copper, manganese), TDS, nitrates, phosphates, and confirmatory readings of standard parameters. LaMotte, Hach, and many pool supply companies offer mail-in lab services.

When Lab Testing Is Warranted

  • Suspected metal contamination (green or brown staining not explained by algae)
  • Customer disputes your field readings (lab results are defensible)
  • CYA above 100 ppm where turbidity test accuracy degrades
  • Commercial pool compliance documentation
  • Opening a new pool fill or switching to well water supply
  • Persistent unexplained cloudiness, corrosion, or equipment damage

The Professional Toolkit Recommendation

For a service truck serving residential pools, the practical recommendation:

  • Primary: Taylor K-2006 FAS-DPD Complete Kit — use for every account, every visit (FC/CC/pH minimum; full test monthly)
  • Secondary: Aquachek Select or Taylor 3-way strips — for a quick visual check between full tests or when time is critical
  • Consider adding: LaMotte ColorQ Pro 9 digital reader — reduces subjective error and speeds up testing if you have 20+ accounts per route
  • Establish a lab relationship: Have a certified lab on file for metal tests and disputed readings

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the most accurate pool water test?

For field testing, the Taylor K-2006 FAS-DPD drop test kit is the most accurate widely available method for free chlorine, combined chlorine, pH, alkalinity, calcium hardness, and CYA. For absolute accuracy — particularly for metals — a certified lab water analysis is the gold standard. Digital photometric readers are accurate for most parameters with less subjective error.

Are pool test strips accurate enough for professional use?

Quality test strips are acceptably accurate for quick screening at normal parameter ranges. They are ±0.5 pH and ±5–10% for most parameters when fresh and used correctly. However, they wash out at high chlorine levels (above 5–8 ppm), cannot detect combined chlorine separately, and are more susceptible to color vision differences and lighting conditions than drop tests.

What test kit do professional pool techs use?

The Taylor K-2006 FAS-DPD Complete Test Kit is the standard professional reference kit. It measures FC (with FAS-DPD method accurate to high levels), CC, pH, TA, CH, and CYA. LaMotte ColorQ Pro 9 is the professional digital alternative for technicians who prefer photometric readings.

When should I send pool water to a lab?

Send pool water to a lab when you need to test metals beyond your test kit capabilities, a customer disputes your chemistry readings, you need CYA confirmation at levels above 100 ppm, or the pool has unusual chemistry issues that field tests don't explain.
