Buying a Pool Route: What to Look For and What to Avoid

📅 December 13, 2025Pool Pro⏱ 7 min read

Buying a pool service route is one of the fastest paths into the pool service business. Instead of spending 6–12 months building an account base from scratch, you acquire existing, paying customers with established service histories. But pool route purchases can go very wrong if you don't know what to look for. This guide covers the full due diligence process that experienced operators use.

Valuation: How Much Should You Pay?

The standard valuation multiple for residential pool service routes is 8–12x monthly billing. This is the industry convention, not EBITDA-based valuation (which is more relevant for multi-tech business acquisitions).

Example:

  • 25-account route at $190/month average = $4,750/month billing
  • At 10x: $47,500 purchase price
  • At 8x (below-average): $38,000 — appropriate if there are retention risks
  • At 12x (premium): $57,000 — warranted for a route with written contracts, strong retention, and premium-priced accounts

Factors that justify a premium multiple:

  • Written service agreements (not just verbal relationships)
  • Accounts priced at or above market rate (not $120/month in a $180 market)
  • Customer average tenure of 3+ years
  • Geographic density (minimal drive time between stops)
  • Equipment in good condition at each pool

Factors that should push the multiple lower:

  • Multiple customer cancellations in the last 6 months
  • Accounts priced significantly below market (seller kept prices low to maintain headcount)
  • No written contracts — accounts can leave the next day
  • Route is geographically scattered, adding significant drive time

Due Diligence: What to Verify

Financial Records

Request 12 months of bank statements or invoicing records showing all service deposits. This verifies: actual account count (vs claimed), actual billing amounts, consistency of payments, and absence of seasonal gaps that would indicate customers who cancelled. A seller who resists providing bank statements is a red flag.

Customer List and Tenure

Get the full customer list with names, addresses, service day, monthly billing amount, and the date service started. Calculate the average customer tenure — anything below 18 months suggests a churn problem. Count how many customers have been on the route for 3+ years — this is the stable core that determines long-term route value.

The Route-Along

Before closing, ride the full route with the seller on at least two service days. You're observing:

  • Actual pool conditions — are they well-maintained or perpetually struggling?
  • Customer relationships — does the seller get a warm reception?
  • Drive time between stops — does the route pencil at the claimed daily account count?
  • Equipment condition — are pools on aging single-speed pumps, failing filters, corroded heaters?

If a seller won't allow a route-along before closing, walk away. A legitimate seller has nothing to hide. Reluctance to do a route-along is the clearest signal that something about the account base doesn't match what's represented in the asking price.

Customer Introduction Letter

Negotiate a seller-signed introduction letter that goes to every customer before the transition, introducing you and endorsing the service handoff. This alone can reduce first-90-day cancellation rates by 30–40%. Customers who cancel often do so because the transition feels abrupt and impersonal.

What to Include in the Purchase Agreement

Never close on a pool route without a written purchase agreement that includes:

  • Customer list: Complete, with billing amounts and start dates
  • Service agreements: Assignment of existing agreements to you
  • Equipment list: Any tools, chemical inventory, or equipment included in the sale
  • Non-compete clause: Seller agrees not to start a competing service within a defined geographic radius for 2–3 years
  • Transition period: Paid training period (1–4 weeks) where seller introduces you to customers
  • Retention guarantee: If X% of accounts cancel within the first 90 days, seller refunds a proportional amount of the purchase price. Standard terms: 10% cancellation triggers a partial refund, calculated per-account.

Finding Routes for Sale

Pool routes are rarely listed publicly. The primary sources:

  • Industry brokers: Pool Route Brokers, Pool Routes Pro, and regional brokers specifically list pool service routes
  • Direct networking: Tell every pool supply distributor rep you know that you're looking. They hear about retirements and sales before anyone else.
  • Cold outreach: Identify operators in your target geography (business license records, Google Maps service listings) and send a professional letter expressing interest in buying if they ever consider selling
  • Facebook groups: Pool service tech groups occasionally have for-sale listings, and asking directly gets responses

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the standard price multiple for a pool service route?

Pool service routes traditionally sell at 8–12x monthly billing. A route generating $8,000/month in service billing typically sells for $64,000–$96,000. Routes with strong retention history and written contracts command the higher end.

How do I verify a pool route's income before buying?

Request 12 months of bank statements or invoicing records, a customer list with billing amounts and tenure dates, and references from at least 3–5 customers. Drive the route with the seller and verify the accounts physically.

What are the biggest red flags when buying a pool route?

Red flags include: high customer turnover in the last 6 months, verbal-only service agreements, prices significantly below market rate, and a seller who won't allow a route-along before closing.

Should I hire the seller to train me after buying a pool route?

Yes — negotiate a 2–4 week paid transition period where the seller introduces you to customers and explains the specific quirks of each pool. Customer introduction dramatically improves retention in the first 90 days.

What should be included in a pool route purchase agreement?

The purchase agreement should include: customer list with contact information, service agreements assigned to you, equipment list, non-compete clause, transition period terms, and a retention guarantee with seller-backed refund if accounts cancel within 60–90 days.
