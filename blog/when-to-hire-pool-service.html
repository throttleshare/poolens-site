When to Hire a Pool Service: The Signs You Need Professional Help

📅 April 5, 2026Pool Lifestyle⏱ 6 min read

Every pool owner starts with good intentions. But life intervenes — vacation, busy seasons, summer heat waves — and small chemistry problems become big ones. Knowing the specific signals that indicate you're past DIY territory isn't defeatist: it's the difference between a $150 service call and a $600 green pool recovery. Here are the situations where calling a professional is the economically rational choice.

Chemistry Emergencies: When to Call Now

Green or Black Algae

Green water (algae bloom) can be handled DIY with the right knowledge and chemical quantities. But black algae — the dark spots that form on plaster and pebble surfaces — is significantly harder to treat. Black algae has a waxy coating that protects it from normal chlorine levels. It requires physical brushing with a stainless steel brush, superchlorination at 20+ PPM, and multiple treatment cycles. Attempting black algae treatment without the right process often results in spending $200 in chemicals to achieve partial results. A professional who treats black algae regularly brings the right protocol and the right concentration on the first visit.

Persistent Cloudiness After Treatment

If you've added chemicals based on test readings and the pool is still cloudy after 48 hours with the filter running, something is wrong with the diagnosis. Either the testing was inaccurate, the chemistry correction was off, or there's a filter issue preventing clearance. This is a diagnostic situation — not a "more chemicals" situation. Adding more chemicals to a miscalculated pool usually makes it worse. A professional with a pool store analyzer and diagnostic experience can identify what's actually driving the cloudiness.

CYA Over 100 PPM with Chemistry Not Responding

When CYA is very high, chlorine becomes progressively less effective — a phenomenon called "chlorine lock." A pool with CYA at 120–150 PPM may not clear even with aggressive chlorination because the stabilizer has impaired the chlorine's ability to sanitize. The fix requires dilution (partial drain and refill). If you're adding chlorine and getting no apparent response, have your CYA tested professionally before spending more on chemicals.

Seasonal Services Best Left to Professionals

Spring Opening

Spring pool opening involves: removing and storing the cover properly, reinstalling equipment (heater, chlorinator, automation), pressurizing the system, inspecting for winter damage, and establishing startup chemistry. The inspection component — checking for freeze damage, cracked pipes, damaged O-rings, and equipment wear over winter — requires experience to do thoroughly. Missing a cracked fitting on opening day leads to a water loss problem that costs far more than the opening service.

Fall Closing

Winterizing a pool correctly requires blowing out all plumbing lines, adding antifreeze to equipment, plugging return lines and skimmer, adjusting chemistry for winter balance, and securing the cover properly. Missed steps in closing routinely result in freeze damage to plumbing or equipment — repairs that cost $500–$3,000. In climates where hard freezes occur, professional closing is worth every dollar.

How to Choose a Pool Service Professional

Not all pool service companies are equal. When evaluating a service provider:

  • Ask for proof of insurance — both general liability and worker's compensation
  • Ask if technicians hold CPO certification (Certified Pool Operator, issued by the Pool and Hot Tub Alliance)
  • Get a written service agreement specifying what's included in monthly service
  • Ask specifically how they handle chemistry — what test kit or method they use and how readings are recorded
  • Ask for references from clients they've served for 2+ years

Frequently Asked Questions

Why is my pool still cloudy after adding chemicals?

Persistent cloudiness after chemical addition usually means either the chemistry correction was wrong (treating the symptom not the cause), the filter is clogged and not clearing particles, or there's an ongoing source of cloudiness (algae bloom, calcium precipitation, or very high TDS). If cloudiness persists after 24 hours of running the filter and correct chemistry, call a professional.

How long does it take for a pool to clear after treatment?

After correct chemical treatment and with a clean, running filter, a mildly cloudy pool should clear in 24–72 hours. A seriously green pool typically requires 3–7 days of aggressive treatment, running the filter 24/7, and multiple backwash or cleaning cycles before water clarity is restored.

What does it cost to have a pool professionally opened for the season?

Spring pool opening service typically costs $150–$400 depending on region, pool size, and what's included. Basic opening includes removing the cover, reinstalling equipment, turning on the system, testing chemistry, and adding startup chemicals. More complete service includes filter cleaning, equipment inspection, and balancing to perfect chemistry.

How do I find a good pool service company?

Look for pool service companies certified by the Pool and Hot Tub Alliance (PHTA) — the CPO (Certified Pool Operator) credential indicates formal training. Ask for references from current clients, verify licensing and insurance, and get written pricing for what's included in monthly service. Avoid companies that can't explain their chemistry process.

What does weekly pool service include?

Standard weekly pool service includes: water chemistry testing and adjustment, skimming of surface debris, brushing walls and steps, vacuuming or confirming robotic cleaner operation, emptying skimmer baskets, checking equipment operation, and filter pressure check. Chemical cost may be included or billed separately depending on the service agreement.
