Testing for Pool Phosphates: Kits, Methods, and When to Test

📅 April 9, 2026Products⏱ 6 min read

Phosphates are a frequently misunderstood parameter in pool chemistry. Pool stores market phosphate removers aggressively, sometimes creating the impression that phosphates are the primary cause of algae. The reality is more nuanced. This guide covers what phosphates actually do in pool water, when testing is warranted, and which test kits give accurate results.

Phosphates in Pool Chemistry: What They Actually Do

Phosphates are nutrients — specifically, they're the primary limiting nutrient for algae growth in many aquatic environments. Algae needs phosphates to grow. In a pool where chlorine is adequate (2+ PPM free chlorine at appropriate CYA levels), phosphates alone won't cause an algae outbreak because the chlorine kills algae faster than it can grow. The phosphate concern becomes real in two situations:

  1. Chronically marginal chlorine: A pool running at 0.5–1.0 PPM free chlorine (often from high CYA impairing effectiveness) gives algae a growth window. High phosphates in this scenario significantly amplify the algae problem.
  2. Very high phosphate levels (1,000+ PPB): At extreme phosphate concentrations, algae can grow fast enough to challenge even adequate chlorine levels, particularly in warm weather.

The practical takeaway: if chlorine is properly managed, phosphates are rarely the limiting factor in algae control. If you're treating recurring algae with correct chemistry and it keeps coming back, phosphates are worth investigating.

Phosphate test strips give ballpark readings but lack the precision needed for accurate dosing of phosphate remover. When to Test for Phosphates

Test phosphate levels in these situations:

  • Recurring algae despite correct chemistry: If algae keeps returning despite adequate free chlorine and correct CYA, phosphates may be the feeding source allowing algae to persist below treatment thresholds
  • Pools under heavy tree cover: Leaf debris is a major phosphate source; annual spring testing establishes the baseline after winter decomposition
  • Post-spring opening: Phosphates accumulate over the winter; opening the season with a phosphate baseline tells you whether removal is needed before swim season starts
  • High-traffic pools in lawn care season: Fertilizer runoff near pools is a significant phosphate loading event
  • Before recommending phosphate removal products: Never add phosphate remover without testing first — some "elevated" phosphate concerns are actually within normal range

How to Use Phosphate Remover

If phosphate levels exceed 500 PPB (especially with a history of algae issues), phosphate removal is appropriate. Products like Natural Chemistry PHOSfree, In The Swim Phosphate Remover, and BioGuard Phosphate Remover use rare earth metal compounds (typically lanthanum) that react with phosphates and cause them to precipitate out of solution.

Application protocol:

  1. Balance chemistry first — pH, chlorine, and alkalinity in range
  2. Add phosphate remover per label dose (based on water volume and initial phosphate level)
  3. Run filter continuously for 24–48 hours — the water will temporarily cloud as phosphate particles precipitate
  4. Clean the filter (cartridge hose, sand backwash, or DE teardown) after treatment — the precipitated phosphates collect in the filter media
  5. Retest phosphates — if still elevated, repeat with a reduced dose

Frequently Asked Questions

Do pool phosphates cause algae?

Phosphates are a nutrient source for algae — they don't directly cause algae, but high phosphate levels (above 500 PPM) make algae treatment more difficult and can contribute to persistent algae problems in pools where chlorine levels are borderline adequate. A pool with adequate free chlorine (2–3 PPM) and normal CYA typically controls algae even with moderate phosphate levels.

What is a good phosphate level for a pool?

Most pool chemistry references consider phosphate levels below 200 PPB (parts per billion) acceptable. Levels between 200–500 PPB are elevated but manageable with good chlorine maintenance. Above 500 PPB, phosphate removal treatment becomes advisable, particularly in pools with a history of recurring algae problems.

What is the best pool phosphate test kit?

Top pool phosphate test kits include: LaMotte Phosphate Test Kit ($25–$35, high accuracy, 0–1,000 PPB range), Natural Chemistry Phosfree test strips ($8–$12, quick but less precise), and Taylor Phosphate Comparator ($30–$40, professional-grade accuracy). Pool store digital analyzers also test phosphates at varying accuracy levels.

Where do pool phosphates come from?

Pool phosphates come from multiple sources: municipal water supplies (phosphate is added as a corrosion inhibitor), fertilizer runoff entering the pool, leaves and organic debris decomposing in the water, bather loads (sweat and urine contain phosphates), and some pool chemicals — particularly some algaecides and stain treatments.

How do you remove phosphates from pool water?

Phosphate removers (lanthanum-based products like Natural Chemistry PHOSfree, BioGuard Pool Magnet Plus for phosphates, or In The Swim Phosphate Remover) precipitate phosphates out of solution. They cause temporary cloudiness as phosphate particles drop out of solution and are captured by the filter. Run the filter 24 hours after treatment and clean the filter after the cloudiness clears.
