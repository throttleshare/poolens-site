How to Read Pool Test Strips Accurately

📅 December 24, 2025Chemistry⏱ 5 min read

Pool test strips are the most commonly used chemistry measurement tool in residential pool service — and they're also the most commonly misused. Bad technique produces bad data, and bad data produces bad chemical decisions. For homeowners managing their own pool chemistry, understanding the limitations and proper technique of test strips is the difference between a well-maintained pool and a constant chemistry struggle. For pool service techs, knowing how to explain strip limitations to customers is part of professional communication.

The Correct Test Strip Technique

Most strip testing errors come from technique problems, not defective strips. The correct procedure:

  1. Sample location: Collect water at elbow depth, at least 18 inches from returns, skimmer, and any chemical introduction points. Surface water and water near returns have different chemistry from the main pool body.
  2. Dip time: Hold the strip in the water for exactly the time specified on the label — typically 2–5 seconds for most brands. Not longer. Longer exposure changes some pad colors.
  3. Removal: Remove the strip and hold it horizontally, not vertically. Tilting allows test pad chemicals to run into each other, contaminating adjacent pads.
  4. Read immediately: Read within 15 seconds of removal. Colors continue to develop after removal — readings taken at 30 seconds or later are less accurate. Some pads stabilize faster than others; always read FC/chlorine pads first.
  5. Lighting: Read in bright, natural daylight. Indoor incandescent lighting (yellow-warm light) significantly distorts color perception. Fluorescent lighting is better but not ideal. If inside, stand in front of a window.
  6. Compare to the bottle: Compare the strip to the color chart on the bottle you're currently using — not a separate chart, a photocopy, or a phone photo. The chart on the bottle is calibrated to that specific lot of strips.

The most common strip-reading error: comparing colors to a label that's faded or wet. The label on the test strip bottle gets water on it and degrades with the sun. Once the color chart is difficult to read clearly, replace the container — the strips inside may still be fine, but an unreadable chart makes accurate readings impossible.

The CYA Strip Problem

CYA measurement on test strips deserves specific attention: it's the one parameter where strips are genuinely unreliable in a way that can cause real harm. CYA strips use a color reaction that is notoriously sensitive to interference from pool water contaminants, pH, and strip age. Experienced techs routinely see CYA strips report 50 ppm in pools that reagent tests show at 120 ppm — or vice versa.

For CYA specifically: always use the turbidity comparison method. The Taylor K-2006 CYA test uses a melamine reagent that creates a turbid solution; you match the turbidity level to a comparison tube. It's a physical measurement, not a color comparison, and is significantly more reliable. Never make a drain-and-refill decision based on a strip CYA reading.

The Chlorine Bleach-Out Problem

Standard DPD test strips bleach out at FC above approximately 5–10 ppm — when the pink color fades to white or light pink, the reading appears lower than actual. This is the most dangerous strip error: after shocking a pool to 20+ ppm FC, a strip might read 3–4 ppm. A tech who trusts this reading might add unnecessary chlorine.

The workaround: if a strip reads near-zero FC immediately after shocking, the pool is either shocked correctly and the strip bleached out, or the pool genuinely isn't holding chlorine (which requires investigation, not more shocking). Test with a FAS-DPD kit (Taylor K-2006) to get accurate high-FC readings.

Strip Storage and Expiration

  • Store strips at room temperature — never in a hot truck cab, glove compartment, or direct sun. Heat degrades the impregnated reagents
  • Store in the original container with the desiccant cap — humidity degrades strips significantly
  • Check the expiration date — expired strips can read consistently high or low; replace annually at minimum
  • Never touch the test pads with wet fingers before use — skin oils and water contaminate the pad chemistry
  • Never pour unused strips back into the container — contamination from handling ruins the remaining strips

When to Upgrade Beyond Strips

Strips are appropriate for: routine monitoring when the pool is in good shape and you want to verify it hasn't changed dramatically. They are not appropriate for: diagnosing chemistry problems, making large chemical additions, evaluating new accounts, or any situation where accuracy matters.

Frequently Asked Questions

How accurate are pool test strips?

Pool test strips have ±0.5 pH and ±0.5 ppm FC accuracy under ideal conditions. Real-world accuracy is often worse due to lighting conditions, user color perception, expired or contaminated strips, and the limited color scale resolution. They're useful for quick monitoring but insufficient for precise professional chemistry decisions.

What is the correct way to use pool test strips?

Dip at elbow depth away from returns, hold for exactly the labeled time (usually 3–5 seconds), remove and hold horizontal, read in bright natural light within 15 seconds of removal, compare to the color chart on the current bottle. Each of these steps materially affects reading accuracy.

Why do my pool test strips give wrong readings?

Common causes: reading in poor lighting (yellow artificial light distorts colors), reading after the indicated time window, expired or contaminated strips, and testing near returns where chemistry concentrations differ from the main pool. Bleach-out at high FC (above 10 ppm) makes strips read falsely low.

Are 5-way or 7-way test strips better?

More parameters aren't always better if accuracy suffers. CYA strips in multi-parameter tests are notoriously inaccurate. For professional use, a reliable FC+pH 2-strip used with a dedicated CYA turbidity test provides better information than a 7-in-1 that compromises accuracy on every parameter.

When should I use a liquid test kit instead of strips?

Use a liquid test kit when diagnosing chemistry problems, when FC is above 10 ppm, when making significant chemical additions, or any time accuracy is critical. Strips are for monitoring; liquid kits (Taylor K-2006) are for diagnosis and precision.
