How to Lower CYA in Your Pool (Spoiler: Drain and Dilute)

September 13, 2025 Chemistry 8 min read

High CYA is one of the most common and most frustrating pool chemistry problems — and it has exactly one reliable solution. Despite what some pool stores sell and what some YouTube videos claim, there is no effective chemical method for reducing cyanuric acid at pool scale. This guide covers how to calculate the correct drain-and-refill volume, when reverse osmosis is a better option, and what to watch for on fill water that might cause CYA to creep up again.

Why CYA Can't Be Chemically Removed

Cyanuric acid (isocyanuric acid, 1,3,5-triazine-2,4,6-triol) is a very stable organic compound. Unlike chlorine, which reacts rapidly with organic matter and breaks down in sunlight, CYA is highly resistant to chemical degradation in pool conditions. It doesn't significantly react with chlorine at pool pH. It isn't broken down by UV light at practical exposure levels. It doesn't precipitate or settle out of solution at pool chemistry ranges.

Products marketed as "CYA reducers" or "stabilizer reducers" typically claim one of two mechanisms:

  • Enzymatic or biological degradation: Microorganisms capable of breaking down cyanuric acid (Pseudomonas sp. and others) do exist in soil environments. However, maintaining a viable population at pool scale, at pool chlorine concentrations, at pool temperatures, while also oxidizing the necessary chemical load, is not practical.
  • Oxidative degradation: CYA does slowly degrade through oxidation over very long timescales, but the rate at pool conditions is negligible compared to the accumulation rate from trichlor maintenance.
The honest answer when a customer asks: "There is no chemical you can add to lower CYA. It is like trying to remove salt by adding a different chemical to the water — the only way to reduce it is to remove some water and replace it with water that doesn't have CYA in it."

The Drain-and-Refill Method

CYA reduction through dilution follows a simple linear relationship. The percentage of pool volume drained equals the percentage reduction in CYA (assuming the fill water has zero CYA, which is true for most municipal water).

If Fill Water Contains CYA

Municipal water typically contains no CYA. However, in some areas, source water from ponds, lakes, or reclaimed water sources may contain low-level organic compounds including triazines. If you suspect the fill water has CYA, test it. Well water is almost always CYA-free; municipal surface water should be tested seasonally in areas where agricultural runoff is a concern.

Warning — partial drains on fiberglass and vinyl liner pools: Draining more than 30–40% of a vinyl liner pool risks liner fold and wrinkle damage as the liner loses support pressure from the water. Draining a fiberglass pool more than 30% risks hydrostatic pressure from groundwater floating the shell. Consult with the pool owner about soil drainage conditions and liner age before recommending large partial drains. For aggressive drains (50%+), drain slowly (over 2–3 days) while monitoring conditions.

How to Execute a Partial Drain and Refill

  1. Calculate the exact volume to drain using the table above
  2. Set up a waste line (backwash valve to waste, submersible pump, or drain line) to direct drained water away from the pool area and into appropriate disposal (storm drain, city sewer per local regulations, landscaping)
  3. Mark the water level on the wall with a piece of tape before draining — this helps you estimate when you've removed the target volume
  4. Drain slowly — no faster than 1,000–2,000 gallons per hour on most residential pools. This prevents rapid hydrostatic pressure changes
  5. Begin refilling before pool is completely at drain target level — use simultaneous drain-and-fill for large drains to maintain equipment protection and structural support
  6. Rebalance chemistry completely — pH, alkalinity, calcium, and chlorine all change significantly with a large volume exchange. Do a full test before and after
  7. Retest CYA after 48 hours — allows time for full mixing and accurate measurement

Reverse Osmosis: The Water-Conserving Alternative

In drought-restricted areas, water-limited climates, or situations where a large drain is inadvisable (vinyl liner, fiberglass, high water table), reverse osmosis filtration is a viable alternative to draining.

A mobile RO unit connected to the pool can reduce CYA from 150 ppm to 30 ppm without removing any significant pool volume. The RO process also simultaneously reduces TDS, nitrates, phosphates, calcium hardness, and most dissolved minerals — essentially producing a water chemistry "reset" without discarding any water.

  • Cost: Typically $400–800 per treatment for a residential pool, depending on pool size and local service pricing
  • Time: 6–12 hours for a 15,000-gallon pool
  • Water preserved: All pool water stays in the pool
  • Limitations: Not available in all markets; requires a service company with RO equipment

Preventing CYA Creep Going Forward

After correcting high CYA, prevent it from creeping up again by:

  • Switching from trichlor tablets to liquid chlorine or cal-hypo for primary chlorination
  • Testing CYA monthly (not quarterly) in tablet-fed pools
  • Using liquid chlorine exclusively for SLAM treatments (never dichlor during SLAM)
  • For SWG pools: targeting CYA at 60–80 ppm rather than 80–100 ppm, giving more headroom before correction is needed

Frequently Asked Questions

How do I lower CYA in my pool?

The only reliable methods for lowering CYA in a pool are partial drain-and-refill with lower-CYA water, or reverse osmosis filtration. No chemical product reliably reduces CYA at pool scale. To lower CYA by 50%, drain 50% of pool water and refill with CYA-free fill water.

Do CYA reducers actually work?

No pool chemical product reliably reduces CYA at pool scale. Some products claim to degrade cyanuric acid using biological or enzymatic processes, but peer-reviewed pool chemistry research has not validated these claims at practical pool doses. The only methods that reliably reduce CYA are draining and reverse osmosis filtration.

How much do I need to drain to lower CYA?

CYA reduction is linear with dilution. To lower CYA by 25%, drain 25% and refill. To lower by 50%, drain 50% and refill. Formula: % to drain = (current CYA - target CYA) / current CYA × 100. For a 15,000-gallon pool needing to go from 100 ppm to 30 ppm, drain approximately 70% (10,500 gallons).

What CYA level requires a drain?

Most pool professionals recommend a drain when CYA exceeds 90–100 ppm for outdoor chlorine pools, or 80–100 ppm in SWG pools. Above these levels, the minimum effective free chlorine becomes impractical to maintain safely and consistently.
