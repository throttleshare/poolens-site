Ozone Pool Systems: Are They Worth the Investment?

📅 December 21, 2025Equipment⏱ 6 min read

Ozone has been used to sanitize public water supplies for over a century — many European municipal water systems use ozone as a primary treatment. In pool water, ozone is approximately 50 times more powerful as an oxidizer than chlorine and destroys bacteria, viruses, chloramines, and organic compounds on contact. It also reverts to oxygen within seconds, leaving no residual chemicals. The technology is legitimate. But pool ozone systems are not a magic bullet, and misunderstanding what they do and don't accomplish leads to expensive disappointment.

The Two Types of Pool Ozone Systems

UV Ozone Systems

UV ozone generators use a UV lamp at 185nm wavelength to convert oxygen molecules (O2) to ozone (O3). They're simpler, less expensive ($300–$800 for the unit), and require less maintenance than corona discharge systems. The drawback: UV ozone production rates are relatively low. For residential pools up to 25,000 gallons, UV ozone systems are generally adequate as a supplement to chlorine.

Corona Discharge (CD) Systems

CD ozone generators create ozone by passing air or pure oxygen through a high-voltage electrical field — the same mechanism as lightning creating ozone smell after a storm. CD systems produce significantly higher ozone concentrations and are more efficient per watt of energy consumed. They require dry air input (a desiccant air dryer in humid climates) and have higher initial cost ($800–$2,500+ for residential units). CD systems are the standard for commercial pool applications and larger residential pools.

Leading manufacturers: Del Ozone (the most widely distributed residential and commercial brand in North America), Mazzei Injector Corporation, Prozone Water Products, and ClearWater Tech.

How Ozone Is Injected

Ozone cannot be pumped — it must be injected into the water stream using a venturi injector or fine-bubble diffuser. The venturi creates a pressure differential that draws ozone into the water, where it reacts with contaminants in a reaction chamber before returning to the pool. Proper sizing of the injector and reaction chamber is critical — underpowered injection means ozone off-gasses before reacting.

System placement: ozone is injected on the return side, after filtration. This allows the ozone to contact the cleanest possible water and react with dissolved organics and pathogens before re-entering the pool.

What Ozone Accomplishes

  • Pathogen destruction: Bacteria and viruses are killed on contact with O3 — ozone is effective against chlorine-resistant organisms including Cryptosporidium and Giardia
  • Chloramine destruction: Combined chlorine compounds are oxidized by ozone, eliminating the pool odor and eye irritation associated with chloramine buildup
  • Organic oxidation: Body oils, cosmetics, sunscreen, urine nitrogen compounds — ozone oxidizes them on contact, reducing the chlorine demand that these contaminants would otherwise create
  • Water clarity: Oxidizing organic compounds improves water clarity and reduces filter loading

The most measurable customer benefit of ozone is usually reported within the first week of operation: water that "doesn't smell like a pool," eyes that don't irritate, and skin that doesn't feel dry after swimming. These are the chloramine reduction effects. Customers who complain about these issues — and whose chemistry is otherwise correct — are the best candidates for an ozone upgrade recommendation.

What Ozone Cannot Do

Ozone decomposes to oxygen within seconds of contact with water. This means no residual sanitation at the pool surface. Every bather who enters the water, every rain event, every leaf that falls in — none of these are sanitized by the ozone system, only by the chlorine residual. A minimum FC of 0.5–1.0 ppm must always be maintained on ozone-treated pools.

Ozone also cannot substitute for balanced water chemistry. pH, TA, CH, and CYA still require management on ozone-equipped pools — ozone handles the biological and organic oxidation load, not the water balance parameters.

Ozone system maintenance is straightforward but cannot be skipped. UV lamp replacement (annually, ~$50–100), desiccant dryer bead replacement on CD systems (annually in humid climates, ~$30–80), and periodic injector cleaning are the primary service items. Most pool techs can add ozone system servicing to their repertoire within a single manufacturer training session.

The Bottom Line for Service Techs

Ozone systems are worth recommending to customers who: have high bather loads (busy family pools), complain about chloramine-related irritation, want to reduce chemical use for environmental or health reasons, or have commercial pools requiring the highest-quality water treatment. The installation is a legitimate revenue opportunity at $700–$3,000+ depending on system choice, and the annual service component adds recurring revenue.

Frequently Asked Questions

How does an ozone pool system work?

Ozone pool systems generate ozone (O3) via corona discharge or UV light and inject it into pool water. Ozone is 50x more powerful as an oxidizer than chlorine and destroys bacteria, viruses, and organic compounds on contact, then reverts to oxygen within seconds.

Can ozone replace chlorine in a swimming pool?

No. Ozone decomposes rapidly and has no residual effect in pool water. A chlorine residual of 0.5–1.0 ppm must always be maintained. Ozone dramatically reduces the chlorine needed to maintain sanitation but is supplemental, not a replacement.

What is the difference between corona discharge and UV ozone systems?

Corona discharge (CD) systems produce higher ozone concentrations and suit larger pools, but cost more and require dry air input. UV ozone systems are simpler and less expensive but produce lower output. For residential pools, UV ozone is usually sufficient; CD is preferred for commercial applications.

How much does an ozone pool system cost?

UV ozone systems for residential pools cost $500–$1,200 installed. Corona discharge systems run $1,100–$3,100+ installed. Del Ozone and Prozone make the most widely distributed residential systems. Ongoing costs include periodic maintenance every 1–3 years.

Is ozone safe for pool swimmers?

Yes, when the system is properly designed. Ozone decomposes to oxygen within seconds and is not present at hazardous concentrations at the pool surface. The equipment area should be vented to manage off-gas — all residential ozone systems are designed with this in mind.
