Pool Replastering: Signs You Need It and What to Expect

📅 October 26, 2025Equipment⏱ 7 min read

Replastering is a $4,000–12,000 renovation decision that pool owners may not fully understand until the symptoms are severe enough to be undeniable. As a service professional, identifying early signs of plaster failure and setting the right expectations is part of the job — both for protecting the client and for generating legitimate renovation referrals.

Signs That a Pool Needs Replastering

Surface Roughness

Healthy plaster is smooth to the touch. As plaster ages and deteriorates, the surface becomes rough — initially like coarse sandpaper, eventually like fine gravel. This roughness is the exposed aggregate from the cement paste that has etched away. Rough plaster damages swimsuits, irritates skin on contact, and provides ideal attachment points for algae (which is why aging plaster pools fight algae harder). When swimmers are consistently complaining about scrapes or suit damage, the surface is past its useful life.

Crazing and Craze Cracking

Fine hairline cracks in a spider-web pattern on the plaster surface — called crazing — indicate the plaster surface layer has become brittle and is beginning to fracture under thermal cycling and water chemistry stress. Crazing itself doesn't immediately cause leaks, but it's a sign that plaster is near end of life. Surface cracks also collect algae and staining that's impossible to remove without replastering.

Chipping, Spalling, and Pop-Offs

When plaster loses bond with the substrate, it chips off. Small chips from isolated mechanical damage (equipment contact, heavy object impact) are isolated problems. Widespread chipping — particularly around fittings, stairs, and low-flow zones — indicates systemic adhesion failure. Plaster chips in the water are abrasive to swimsuits and can damage pump impellers.

Delamination (Hollow Plaster)

Knock on the plaster surface with your knuckles in a systematic pattern around the pool. Properly bonded plaster sounds solid. Delaminated areas produce a hollow "thud" sound. Delaminated sections are plaster that has separated from the gunite shell beneath it — they will eventually crack through and fall off. Extensive delamination is the most definitive indicator that replastering cannot be deferred.

Staining That Cannot Be Removed

Some staining is cosmetic but manageable with chemical treatment. When staining is embedded deeply enough that acid washing, sequestrants, stain erasers, and vitamin C treatments all fail, the staining has penetrated beyond the removable surface layer. This most commonly occurs with iron and copper staining in pools with metal-containing fill water or equipment corrosion.

Lifespan figures assume average water chemistry maintenance. Pools with chronic low pH, high aggressive water (low LSI), or multiple acid washes will see these lifespans cut significantly — sometimes by half.

What Replastering Involves

  1. Drain the pool — fully empty, typically with a submersible pump over 12–24 hours
  2. Chip out existing plaster — mechanically remove all existing plaster down to the gunite shell (chipping tools, jackhammers in severe cases)
  3. Repair the shell — address any cracks, voids, or structural defects in the gunite that are now visible
  4. Bond coat application — a slurry bonding layer to improve adhesion of new plaster to gunite
  5. New plaster application — hand-troweled application of new plaster mix; pool-sized team works continuously to prevent joints and cold spots
  6. Cure and fill — begin filling immediately after plaster is applied; continuous circulation during the first 28-day cure period is critical

Chemistry After Replastering

New plaster is highly alkaline and will dramatically affect fill water chemistry during the curing period. The standard new-plaster startup protocol:

  • First 30 days: run pump 24 hours per day
  • Brush the new plaster surface twice daily for the first 2 weeks — this removes the plaster dust (calcium hydroxide) and prevents blotchy curing
  • Do not add CYA for at least 30 days
  • Expect pH and TA to be very high initially; make incremental acid additions and retest frequently
  • Add startup algaecide per the plastering contractor's protocol

The 28-day curing period is the most critical maintenance window of a new plaster installation. Chemistry must be checked daily and adjusted carefully. Improper chemistry during curing causes permanent discoloration, streaking, and premature surface degradation. Frequently Asked Questions

How long does pool plaster last?

Standard white plaster typically lasts 10–15 years with good chemistry maintenance. Pebble surfaces last 20–25+ years. Quartz aggregate surfaces 15–20 years. Poor water chemistry dramatically shortens plaster life.

What is plaster delamination and is it serious?

Delamination is when plaster separates from the gunite shell underneath, creating hollow-sounding areas when tapped. Delaminated sections eventually flake off, creating rough patches and sharp edges. Delamination requires replastering, not repair.

Can you patch pool plaster instead of replastering the whole pool?

Minor chips (less than a few square feet) can be patched. However, patches are always visible as color mismatches. If patching would cover more than 10–15% of the surface, full replastering is the better recommendation for both cosmetics and durability.

What causes pool plaster to deteriorate faster?

The primary accelerant is aggressive water chemistry — pH consistently below 7.2, high TDS, low calcium hardness, and low alkalinity. Over-acid washing also removes surface material. Physical factors include poor original application quality and freeze-thaw cycles.
