Pool Tech Essential Tools: The Kit That Pays for Itself

📅 November 12, 2025Pool Pro⏱ 6 min read

The difference between a professional pool tech and an amateur isn't experience — it's the kit. The right tools make every stop faster, every chemical decision more accurate, and every customer interaction more confident. The wrong tools cost you time, errors, and callbacks that eat your margin.

Here's a complete breakdown of what every pool tech should carry, what's optional, and what's genuinely not worth the money.

Water Testing: The Foundation of Everything

Bad water test = bad chemistry decision = callback. Testing gear is never the place to cut corners.

Taylor K-2006 — The Standard ($65–$80)

The Taylor K-2006 uses the FAS-DPD method for chlorine testing, which is far more accurate than OTO (yellow/orange) kits at higher chlorine levels. It measures free chlorine, combined chlorine (CC), pH, total alkalinity, calcium hardness, and cyanuric acid (CYA). If you're only buying one test kit, this is it. Refill reagents cost $25–$35/year for a typical route.

LaMotte ColorQ Pro 7 — Digital Upgrade ($200–$250)

For techs running 20+ stops per day, a digital photometer eliminates the eye-strain of reading reagent colors. The ColorQ Pro 7 tests 7 parameters and stores readings. The tradeoff: it needs calibration and reagent cartridges ($30–$50 per refill). Worth it at high volume, overkill for a starter kit.

Test Strips — Not for Professionals

Test strips are fine for homeowners who want a quick sanity check. For a professional whose livelihood depends on accurate chemistry, they're unacceptably imprecise — particularly for CYA and calcium hardness. Don't rely on them as your primary testing method.

Chemical Management

Chemical storage tote

A dedicated, ventilated chemical storage tote mounted or secured in your truck bed prevents spills and keeps incompatible chemicals separated. Never store chlorine and acid (muriatic) in the same unseparated bin. A basic tote from Northern Tool or Home Depot runs $40–$80.

Measuring cups and dosing containers

Graduated measuring cups rated for chemical use ($5–$15) and a dedicated powder scoop for each chemical type. Label everything. Cross-contamination from an unclean scoop can cause chemical reactions in the bag or at the pool.

Safety Equipment

This is not optional. Chemical burns and respiratory exposure are real hazards:

  • Chemical-resistant gloves (nitrile or neoprene) — $12–$25/box
  • Safety goggles (not sunglasses) — $15–$30
  • Chemical-resistant apron — $20–$40
  • Eyewash bottle in the truck cab — $8–$15

Equipment Service Tools

Once you're doing more than basic maintenance:

  • Multi-tip screwdriver set — essential for pump lid, filter band, and timer access
  • Slip-joint pliers — filter head removal, union joints
  • Pipe tape (Teflon) — any threaded fitting repair
  • Pressure gauge — filter PSI verification without relying on the built-in gauge
  • Multimeter — motor troubleshooting and heater diagnosis ($25–$50)
  • Impeller cleaning tool — a bent wire or specialty tool for clearing debris from pump impellers

What Not to Buy (Yet)

  • Robotic vacuum: Great for customers, not necessary for your service kit. Some techs deploy a Dolphin or Polaris at specific accounts and charge extra — but it's an upgrade, not a starter item.
  • Spectrophotometer: LaMotte and Hach make excellent $800–$1,200 units. Overkill for residential routes.
  • Bulk chemical storage tanks: Useful at 80+ accounts. Expensive and space-consuming before that.

Start lean, buy quality where it matters (test kit, pole, brushes), and upgrade as revenue grows. The kit that pays for itself fastest is the one that makes you accurate, fast, and professional on every stop — not the one with the most gear.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the best pool water test kit for professionals?

The Taylor K-2006 is the industry standard for FAS-DPD chlorine testing. It accurately measures free and combined chlorine, pH, total alkalinity, calcium hardness, and cyanuric acid. Cost is around $60–$80.

Are digital test meters worth it for pool techs?

Yes, for high-volume routes. A good digital photometer like the LaMotte ColorQ Pro 7 ($200–$250) pays for itself in time saved within a season. However, they require calibration and reagent restocking.

What's the best pool brush for a service tech?

A 18-inch nylon/stainless steel combination brush handles most surfaces. Use all-nylon for plaster and tile, stainless for stubborn algae on gunite. Hayward and ProTec are reliable brands.

Do I need a pressure washer?

Not for basic maintenance. A pressure washer is useful for filter media cleaning (especially DE grids) and deck washing, but it's an upgrade, not a starter item.

What apps do professional pool techs use?

SplashLens for offline dosing calculations and field reference. Skimmer or Pool Brain for route management and customer billing. Many techs use both — SplashLens for chemistry, a route app for scheduling.
