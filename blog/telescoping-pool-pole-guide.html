Telescoping Pool Poles: What Size, What Material, What's Worth It

📅 March 1, 2026Products⏱ 6 min read

A telescoping pool pole is the base tool that everything else attaches to. Get the right one and every attachment — brush, net, vacuum head — works efficiently. Get a cheap one and you'll be fighting a wobbly, slipping pole that adds unnecessary physical effort to every stop on your route.

Material: Aluminum vs Fiberglass

For professional pool service work, fiberglass is the correct material. The non-conductive property alone is worth the price premium — a pool technician working near light fixtures, junction boxes, or any wet electrical equipment should not be handling a conductive aluminum pole. Every major pool service association recommends non-conductive poles as the professional standard.

Length: What Do You Actually Need?

Telescoping poles extend from their collapsed length to their maximum length. Common residential poles collapse to 4–6 feet and extend to 8–16 feet. For the widest reach on standard residential pools, a 8–16 foot pole (16-foot extended) handles virtually every residential application. Commercial pools, lap pools, or extra-deep pools may need 21-foot poles.

A pole that's too short causes you to lean over the water or reach awkwardly, which is both inefficient and a fall risk. A pole that's too long is unwieldy and tiring. For a service route that includes a variety of pool sizes, the 8–16 foot range is the standard pick.

Top Telescoping Poles for Pool Service

Poolmaster Professional 8–16' Fiberglass — Best All-Around

The Poolmaster fiberglass professional pole is the industry workhorse. It's light enough to use all day without arm fatigue, the cam lock holds extension reliably under working load, and the tip connection is the standard 1-1/4 inch locking pin that fits every attachment. At around $45–$55, it's a legitimate professional tool priced reasonably. For a service company, buy enough for every technician and replace on a scheduled basis rather than running them to failure.

Pentair R111058 Fiberglass Pro Pole — Best Premium Pick

Pentair's professional pole uses an improved cam lock design that's less prone to slipping under heavy brushing loads than most competitors. The fiberglass layup is slightly stiffer at extension, which reduces flex and improves control during vacuuming. At $65–$75, it's the premium option. For lead technicians servicing high-end residential accounts where presentation and professionalism matter, this is worth the upgrade.

Swimline 8016 Aluminum — Best Budget Option

If budget is the primary constraint, the Swimline 8016 anodized aluminum pole delivers adequate performance for residential use at $20–$28. The anodizing reduces corrosion compared to bare aluminum. Keep it away from electrical hazards and replace more frequently than fiberglass. Acceptable for technicians primarily servicing residential pools without electrical proximity hazards.

Never use an aluminum pole near pool lights, junction boxes, pump wiring, or any wet electrical equipment. A single contact with an energized component through a conductive pole can be fatal. Fiberglass is not optional for professional safety standards — it's the minimum.

Locking Mechanisms: What to Look For

The cam lock (also called the Twist-Lok or flip lock depending on brand) is the mechanism that holds the pole at your chosen extension. Quality cam locks engage firmly with a satisfying click and don't slip under downward pressure during brushing or vacuuming. Worn cam locks slip constantly, requiring you to re-lock between strokes — a major time and energy waste.

Signs a pole's lock is failing: the pole slowly collapses during use, or you need to crank it very tight to get any hold. Replace the pole before this happens rather than managing a failing lock all season.

Clean pole connections with fresh water after every service day and wipe the cam lock mechanism dry. Chemical residue and mineral buildup accelerate cam lock wear. A 60-second rinse extends pole life significantly.

Frequently Asked Questions

What length telescoping pole do I need for a residential pool?

A 8–16 foot telescoping pole covers most residential inground pools. For pools wider than 20 feet or deeper than 6 feet, a 16–21 foot pole may be needed to reach the center of the pool floor from the edge. Most service techs carry a standard 16-foot pole for versatility.

What is the standard pool pole connection size?

The industry standard is a 1-1/4 inch outer diameter tip with a locking pin. This fits virtually all brushes, nets, vacuum heads, and other pool tools. Verify this specification before purchasing tools separately from your pole.

Are fiberglass poles better than aluminum for pool service?

Fiberglass poles are non-conductive (critical for safety near electrical equipment), more resistant to corrosion, and lighter for their strength. Aluminum poles are cheaper but conduct electricity and corrode faster in pool chemistry environments. For professionals, fiberglass is the better long-term investment.

How do I keep the locking mechanism on my pole from slipping?

Locking mechanisms slip when the cam lock wears, when the pole sections have minor damage, or when the inner section diameter has corroded unevenly. Clean the sections regularly, lubricate the cam lock with silicone spray, and replace poles when the lock no longer holds reliably under working load.

Can I use one pole for brushing, skimming, and vacuuming?

Yes. A single telescoping pole with a standard 1-1/4 inch tip fits brushes, skimmer nets, and vacuum heads interchangeably. Most service techs use one pole and swap attachments at each pool stop.
