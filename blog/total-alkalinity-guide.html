Total Alkalinity: The Most Misunderstood Pool Parameter

September 2, 2025 Chemistry 9 min read

Ask a homeowner what "alkalinity" means in their pool, and most will say "it's related to pH." They are not wrong — but they are missing the crucial distinction that makes total alkalinity one of the most important parameters a pool tech manages. Getting alkalinity right unlocks stable pH, predictable dosing, and reduced chemical costs. Getting it wrong means fighting pH constantly, burning through acid, and never quite getting the pool balanced.

Total Alkalinity Is Not the Same as pH

This is the foundational concept. pH and total alkalinity are related but distinct measurements:

  • pH measures the current hydrogen ion concentration — how acidic or basic the water is right now
  • Total alkalinity measures the water's buffering capacity — its ability to resist changes in pH

Think of alkalinity as a buffer between pH and the world. A pool with high alkalinity will barely move its pH when acid rain falls, bathers add organic waste, or you add a dose of chlorine. A pool with low alkalinity will swing dramatically in response to the same inputs.

The carbonate buffer system: Total alkalinity in a pool is primarily composed of carbonate (CO₃²⁻), bicarbonate (HCO₃⁻), and hydroxide (OH⁻) ions. These ions neutralize acids by accepting hydrogen ions. The more of these ions present, the stronger the buffer. Sodium bicarbonate (baking soda) adds bicarbonate ions and raises TA; muriatic acid destroys them and lowers TA.

What Happens Outside the Ideal Range

Low Total Alkalinity (Below 60 ppm)

When TA is too low, pH becomes unstable and prone to "bouncing" — large swings in response to small inputs. Specific symptoms:

  • pH drops significantly after a heavy rain event
  • pH rises dramatically after adding trichlor tablets or sodium hypochlorite
  • Chemical doses that previously worked now cause overshoots
  • Chronic pH that is hard to hold stable between visits

Extremely low alkalinity (below 40 ppm) can lead to corrosive water that etches plaster, dissolves grout, pits concrete, and damages metal equipment. Pool water below pH 7.0 combined with low TA is actively destructive.

High Total Alkalinity (Above 150 ppm)

When TA is too high, pH becomes "locked" — resistant to adjustment in either direction. It also tends to push pH upward naturally, because the excess bicarbonate equilibrium drives carbonate formation and CO₂ off-gassing, which raises pH. Symptoms:

  • pH consistently runs high (7.8–8.4) regardless of acid additions
  • Large amounts of acid needed for small pH changes
  • Cloudiness or milky water (calcium carbonate precipitation)
  • Scale formation on tile and equipment at lower temperature ranges

How to Raise Total Alkalinity

Sodium bicarbonate (baking soda, NaHCO₃) is the standard treatment. It raises TA with minimal pH impact at normal doses.

Add sodium bicarbonate by broadcasting around the perimeter of the pool with the pump running. It dissolves quickly and distributes evenly. Large doses (above 10 ppm adjustment) can temporarily cloud water — this clears within 24 hours.

How to Lower Total Alkalinity: The Slug-Dose Aeration Technique

This is where most techs make their biggest alkalinity mistake. When muriatic acid is broadcast across the whole pool, it reduces both pH and TA proportionally. This is useful for lowering pH — but not for specifically targeting high TA without dropping pH below the desired range.

The professional technique for lowering TA while maintaining target pH uses a two-step approach:

Step 1: Slug Dose (Concentrated Acid Addition)

Add the full acid dose in one concentrated location — not broadcast across the pool. Pour it into a single spot in the deep end with the pump temporarily turned off or flowing minimally. The concentrated dose acts on carbonate alkalinity preferentially, destroying more TA per unit of acid than broadcasting does.

Step 2: Aeration Phase

After the acid has dispersed (allow 30 minutes), run all jets, waterfalls, and aerators. Turn on the spa spillover if available. Use a leaf blower to agitate the water surface if needed. Aeration drives dissolved CO₂ out of the water. As CO₂ leaves, pH rises naturally without any chemical input — and without raising TA. Repeat the test and compare pH and TA after 2–4 hours of aeration.

This process takes time: The slug-dose aeration technique for lowering TA is not a one-visit fix for severely elevated alkalinity. A pool at 200 ppm TA that needs to reach 100 ppm may require 3–5 acid treatments with aeration sessions in between. Do not rush it by adding large acid doses all at once — you risk crashing pH below 7.0.

The CYA Interference Problem

Standard alkalinity tests (including the Taylor K-2006 using R-0007 and R-0008) measure total alkalinity including cyanurate alkalinity from CYA. At high CYA levels (above 50 ppm), the test will overread true carbonate alkalinity by approximately 1/3 of the CYA level.

For example, if CYA is at 90 ppm and the test reads 120 ppm TA, the actual carbonate alkalinity is approximately 120 - (90/3) = 90 ppm. This is why pools with high CYA often need to target a lower apparent TA (70–90 ppm) to achieve the same carbonate buffering as a pool with 100 ppm TA and low CYA.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is total alkalinity in a pool and why does it matter?

Total alkalinity (TA) is the water's buffering capacity — its ability to resist pH change. A pool with proper TA (80–120 ppm) holds pH stable between acid additions and chlorine dosing. Low TA causes pH bounce. High TA makes pH hard to adjust and tends to push pH upward over time.

What is the difference between pH and total alkalinity?

pH measures how acidic or basic the water is right now. Total alkalinity measures the water's capacity to resist pH change. A pool can have correct pH but low alkalinity, meaning pH will swing wildly with small inputs. Or it can have high alkalinity and normal pH but pH will be hard to adjust when needed.

How do I lower total alkalinity in a pool?

Lower TA using the slug-dose aeration method: add the full muriatic acid dose in one concentrated area of the pool, which drives down TA more than pH. Then run jets and aeration to drive CO₂ out of the water, which raises pH back to target without raising TA. Repeat as needed.

What happens if pool alkalinity is too low?

Low alkalinity causes pH bounce — large, rapid pH swings in response to small inputs like rain, bather waste, or chemical additions. pH can drop dramatically after a rainstorm in a low-alkalinity pool, making water corrosive to surfaces and equipment.
