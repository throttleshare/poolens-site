Borates in Pool Water: The Underrated Chemistry Secret

September 18, 2025 Pool Chemistry 9 min read

Ask ten pool owners if they've heard of cyanuric acid and most will know it vaguely as "the stabilizer." Ask about borates and you'll get blank stares — or a mention of laundry detergent. Yet pool service professionals who use a borate system routinely report some of the most stable, silky, and trouble-free water they've ever maintained. This is not placebo effect. There is real chemistry behind it.

Borates at 30–50 ppm work on three independent fronts: they buffer pH in a range that pure sodium bicarbonate doesn't cover well, they actively inhibit algae growth without relying on residual chlorine, and they subtly change the surface tension and feel of the water in ways that swimmers notice immediately. If you're not using borates, you're leaving one of pool chemistry's most cost-effective tools on the table.

What Borates Are and How They Work

Borates are compounds containing boron, a naturally occurring element. In pool chemistry, the relevant species is boric acid (H₃BO₃) and its conjugate base tetraborate (B₄O₇²⁻). When dissolved in pool water at a pH of 7.2–7.8, borates exist primarily as undissociated boric acid — a weak acid that provides meaningful buffering capacity in the pH 7.0–9.0 range.

This buffering action is different from — and complementary to — the carbonate buffering system that total alkalinity provides. While total alkalinity primarily resists pH change from acid or base additions, borate buffering specifically resists the slow pH drift caused by CO₂ equilibration, a process that constantly pushes pool pH upward (especially in pools with water features and SWGs).

Why SWG pools benefit especially: Salt water chlorine generators produce sodium hydroxide (NaOH) as a byproduct of electrolysis, creating chronic pH rise. A borate system at 50 ppm significantly slows this drift, reducing how often you need to add acid on SWG accounts.

The Three Benefits of Pool Borates

1. pH Stability

This is the benefit with the clearest chemistry behind it. Boric acid has a pKa of 9.24, which means it provides meaningful buffering capacity across the entire pool-chemistry pH range. Independent studies and decades of service-professional experience show that borate-treated pools drift pH significantly more slowly than non-borate pools — often 30–50% slower, meaning fewer acid calls per season.

2. Algistatic Effect

Borates inhibit algae growth by interfering with photosynthetic enzyme activity. Specifically, boric acid disrupts the Calvin cycle enzymes that algae use for carbon fixation. This is not a kill mechanism — borates don't destroy algae the way chlorine does — but they raise the bar for algae establishment considerably.

In practice: a pool with borates at 50 ppm can experience a brief FC dip or an overcast week without immediately turning green. Without borates, the same event triggers a bloom. For service technicians on weekly routes, this margin matters enormously during hot, busy summer weeks.

3. Water Feel

This is the most subjective benefit but also the one that generates the most word-of-mouth from satisfied pool owners. Borate-treated water feels silkier and softer on the skin, reduces eye irritation, and produces noticeably less of the "stinging eyes" that swimmers associate with a poorly balanced pool (which they often blame on chlorine, but is actually almost always a pH issue).

The mechanism is partly pH stability (stable pH = less irritation) and partly the buffer properties of boric acid itself — which is, notably, an ingredient in contact lens solution for the same reason.

How to Add Borates to a Pool

Method 1: Borax + Muriatic Acid (Most Common)

The standard procedure used by most service professionals:

  1. Calculate the amount of borax needed to reach 30–50 ppm boron. For a 20,000-gallon pool targeting 50 ppm, you need approximately 20 lbs of 20 Mule Team Borax (or 13–14 lbs of sodium tetraborate pentahydrate product).
  2. Pre-dissolve borax in a 5-gallon bucket of warm water, one bag at a time, and pour near the return jets with the pump running.
  3. After borax is distributed (30 minutes), test pH. It will have risen significantly — often to 8.0–8.4.
  4. Add muriatic acid in measured doses to bring pH back to 7.4–7.6. Add acid slowly near the main drain with the pump running; never all at once.
  5. Allow 2–4 hours of circulation, then retest pH and borate level.

Method 2: Dedicated Borate Products

Products like BioGuard Optimizer Plus are formulated as pentahydrate sodium tetraborate rather than decahydrate, meaning less raw material for the same boron dose. They also come with detailed dosing charts. The acid addition requirement is the same, but the math is simpler.

Method 3: Boric Acid Directly

Boric acid granules or powder can be broadcast directly into the pool or dissolved in warm water. This approach avoids the acid-addition step and is pH-neutral, but boric acid dissolves slowly and requires extended circulation. It's most practical for smaller top-up additions rather than initial dosing.

Warning — Acid handling: When adding muriatic acid to offset the pH rise from borax, follow all standard acid safety protocols: add acid to water (never water to acid), dilute before adding to pool, wear splash goggles and acid-resistant gloves. Never pour acid directly onto pool surfaces. See our full muriatic acid guide.

How to Test Borate Levels

Standard Taylor test kits (including the K-2006) do not test for borates. Borate testing requires a dedicated test strip or reagent kit:

  • LaMotte Borate Test Strips — fast, single-step dip test, accurate enough for service work
  • Orenda Technologies borate test — liquid reagent drops, higher precision
  • ProTeam Supreme test strips — included with the product; basic but functional
  • ICP testing (mail-in water analysis) — quantifies boron alongside all other minerals
Test frequency: Because borates don't degrade, you don't need to test at every service call. Test when you add borates initially, then quarterly unless you've done a significant drain and refill. Some technicians test once at startup and once mid-season.

How Long Borates Last in Pool Water

Unlike chlorine, which is consumed by sunlight, organic matter, and oxidation reactions, borates are chemically inert in pool water. They don't evaporate, don't get oxidized, and don't react with other pool chemicals at normal pool pH. The only mechanisms that reduce borate concentration are:

  • Splash-out and evaporation make-up: As water evaporates and is replaced with fresh water, borates are diluted. This is the primary long-term loss mechanism.
  • Backwashing: Each backwash cycle removes some pool water and its dissolved borates. High-backwash-frequency pools may need top-ups more often.
  • Draining: Any partial drain removes borates in proportion to the volume drained.

In a typical outdoor pool with normal evaporation and weekly backwashing, borates may drop 5–10 ppm over a full season. A once-per-season top-up dose is usually sufficient to maintain the target level.

Environmental Considerations

Borates are naturally occurring and present in many soil types, but at elevated concentrations they can inhibit plant growth. The practical concern for service technicians:

  • Don't backwash borate-treated water onto flower beds, vegetable gardens, or sensitive landscaping
  • Discharge to municipal sewer when possible, or onto grass/hardscape
  • At 50 ppm pool concentration, backwash water dilutes significantly before it affects soil — but repeated backwashing in the same spot over a season can accumulate
Warning — Hot tubs and small volumes: Borates concentrate faster in spas and small above-ground pools due to higher evaporation rates relative to volume. Test more frequently and use lower initial doses (targeting 30 ppm rather than 50 ppm) until you've characterized the drift rate for that specific body of water.

Integrating Borates into a Complete Chemistry Program

Borates work best as part of a complete chemistry system, not as a shortcut around proper parameter management. The ideal borate pool still maintains:

  • Free chlorine at appropriate levels for the CYA concentration (7.5–10% of CYA minimum)
  • pH 7.4–7.6
  • Total alkalinity 80–120 ppm
  • Calcium hardness 200–400 ppm
  • CYA appropriate for the sanitizer type (20–50 ppm for liquid chlorine/cal-hypo, 60–80 ppm for SWG)

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the recommended borate level for swimming pools? The most commonly recommended range is 30–50 ppm. Many pool professionals target 50 ppm for maximum pH stability and the strongest algistatic effect. Above 50 ppm, some swimmers may notice eye or skin sensitivity; above 100 ppm, borates can be toxic to plant life from splash-out.
Do borates replace algaecide? Borates are algistatic — they inhibit algae growth — but they are not a substitute for proper chlorine levels. They work best as part of a complete chemistry system. If chlorine drops to zero, algae can still bloom even with borates present. Think of borates as insurance, not replacement.
How do you add borates to a pool? The most common method is using borax (sodium tetraborate decahydrate) combined with muriatic acid to neutralize the pH rise, or using a dedicated borate product like BioGuard Optimizer Plus or ProTeam Supreme. Borax pre-dissolved in warm water and added to the pool with the pump running, followed by acid to correct pH, is the standard approach.
Do borates evaporate or degrade in pool water? No. Unlike chlorine, borates are chemically stable and do not evaporate or get consumed by sunlight, organic matter, or oxidation. The only way borate levels drop is through splash-out, backwashing, draining, or dilution from rain and fresh water additions. In a well-maintained pool, you may only need to top up borates once or twice per season.
Are borates safe for swimmers? At 30–50 ppm, borates are considered safe for swimmers and are non-irritating to skin and eyes. In fact, many swimmers report less eye irritation with borates than without, because borate-buffered water has more stable pH. The concern is not human safety but environmental — don't backwash borate water into gardens.
