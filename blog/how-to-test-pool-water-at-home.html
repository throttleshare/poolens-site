How to Test Pool Water at Home: Test Strips vs Drop Kits

📅 February 9, 2026Pool FAQ⏱ 6 min read
Quick Answer: Test strips are fast and good for routine checks — dip, wait 15 seconds, read the color. Drop kits (DPD type, like Taylor K-2005) are 3–5x more accurate and required when diagnosing problems or calculating chemical doses. Use strips for daily monitoring; use a drop kit when anything is off. Test chlorine and pH 2–3 times per week, alkalinity and calcium monthly.

How to Use Test Strips Correctly

  1. Collect water from 12–18 inches below the surface, away from jets and skimmers
  2. Dip the strip into the water for the time specified on the package (usually 1–3 seconds)
  3. Remove the strip and hold it level — do not shake off excess water
  4. Wait the specified time (usually 15 seconds) before reading — timing matters
  5. Compare to the color chart in natural light — not direct sunlight and not indoor incandescent light
  6. Record results immediately — colors continue to change after reading time

Common strip mistakes: reading too early or too late, holding the strip vertically (water runs and contaminates pads), and using strips from a container that's been left open (humidity degrades reagents). Always cap the container immediately after removing a strip.

How to Use a DPD Drop Kit

Using the Taylor K-2005 (the gold standard residential kit) as an example:

Free Chlorine Test

  1. Fill the large tube to the 25 mL line with pool water
  2. Add 5 drops of R-0001 (DPD #1 reagent) and swirl
  3. Add 5 drops of R-0002 (buffer) and swirl — water turns pink if chlorine is present
  4. Count drops of R-0003 (DPD titrant) until the pink color disappears
  5. Each drop = 0.2 ppm free chlorine

pH Test

  1. Fill the small tube to the 25 mL line with pool water
  2. Add 5 drops of R-0004 (phenol red indicator)
  3. Compare color to the pH reference chart (yellow = low pH, red = high pH)

To measure combined chlorine with a drop kit: subtract the free chlorine reading from the total chlorine reading. The total chlorine test (using DPD #3 in the K-2005) shows all chlorine forms combined. If combined chlorine exceeds 0.3 ppm, breakpoint chlorination is needed.

When to Send Water for Professional Testing

Home test kits cannot measure TDS (total dissolved solids) or metals (iron, copper, manganese). These require a professional water panel, typically available at pool supply stores for $10–$20 or through mail-in services.

Get a professional panel test:

  • At the start of each swimming season
  • When recurring stains appear (metals)
  • When chemistry is chronically difficult to balance (high TDS)
  • When considering a drain and refill

More Pool Questions Answered

What is the best way to test pool water at home?

Test strips for routine daily or weekly monitoring. DPD drop kit (Taylor K-2005) for accurate readings when diagnosing a problem or calculating chemical doses. Drop kits are 3–5x more accurate than strips and can measure combined chlorine, which strips cannot detect reliably.

How accurate are pool test strips?

Strips are accurate to ±0.5–1.0 ppm for chlorine under ideal conditions. Real-world accuracy is lower due to humidity degrading stored strips and color-reading variation. They're useful for trend-checking but not for precise chemical dosing calculations. Verify with a drop kit when chemistry is out of range.

What is a DPD pool test kit?

DPD kits use a reagent that turns pink in proportion to chlorine concentration. You count titration drops to determine exact ppm values. The Taylor K-2005 tests free and total chlorine, pH, alkalinity, calcium hardness, and CYA. Far more precise than strips for problem diagnosis and chemical calculations.

How often should I test my pool water?

Free chlorine and pH: 2–3 times per week in summer, weekly in cooler weather. Alkalinity and calcium: monthly. CYA: monthly. After major events (heavy rain, big pool party, algae treatment): test the next day. Annual professional panel for TDS and metals.

Where should I collect pool water for testing?

12–18 inches below the surface, away from return jets and skimmers. Return jets carry concentrated fresh chlorine. The mid-pool, mid-depth sample gives the most representative reading. Rinse the sample container 3 times with pool water before collecting the final sample.
