Cloudy Pool Water: Diagnosis Guide for Every Cause

November 6, 2025  ·  Chemistry  ·  10 min read

Cloudy pool water is one of the most common service calls, and it is one of the most commonly misdiagnosed. The instinct is to reach for a clarifier or shock and hope for the best. But cloudy water has at least six distinct root causes, and the wrong treatment does nothing — or actively makes things worse.

This guide gives you a structured diagnostic approach: test first, interpret the pattern, then treat the confirmed cause.

Step One: Do a Full Chemistry Panel Before Touching Anything

Treating cloudy water without testing is guessing. Before adding any chemicals, test and record:

  • Free chlorine (FC)
  • Combined chlorine (CC)
  • pH
  • Total alkalinity (TA)
  • Calcium hardness (CH)
  • Cyanuric acid (CYA) if stabilized pool
  • Phosphates (if test kit available)

Also note: when did the water last look clear? Did anything change recently — heavy rain, high bather load, new chemicals, filter service? The history narrows the diagnosis quickly.

The Six Causes of Cloudy Pool Water

Cause 1: Low or Depleted Free Chlorine

This is the most common cause, and the most important to address quickly. When free chlorine drops below the minimum effective level relative to CYA (the FC:CYA ratio), the pool loses sanitation before you can see algae. Bacteria and organic matter accumulate, and the water becomes hazy before it turns green.

Key indicators:

  • FC reads 0–1 ppm (or below 7.5% of CYA level)
  • CC elevated (above 0.3 ppm)
  • Water is hazy, not green — early-stage loss of sanitation

Fix: Shock to breakpoint chlorination. For a stabilized pool, this means raising FC to 40% of CYA or higher with a sustained 8-hour treatment. Run filter continuously.

Cause 2: pH Too High

High pH (above 7.8) is the second most common cause of cloudy water, and it is purely a chemistry issue — the water itself is fine from a sanitation standpoint, but the mineral balance is wrong. At high pH, calcium and carbonate ions combine and precipitate out of solution as microscopic calcium carbonate particles. These particles scatter light and make water appear milky or hazy.

Key indicators:

  • pH reads above 7.8–8.0
  • Chlorine appears adequate but pool is still cloudy
  • No CC elevation
  • Scale buildup may also be visible on tile or equipment

Fix: Lower pH to 7.4–7.6 using muriatic acid or sodium bisulfate (dry acid). As pH drops, the calcium carbonate re-dissolves and water clears within 24–48 hours. No clarifier needed.

Cause 3: High Total Dissolved Solids (TDS) or High Calcium Hardness with High pH

Water that has been in service for years without partial drain-and-refill gradually accumulates dissolved minerals. Very high TDS (above 3,000 ppm in a non-salt pool) reduces water clarity and makes it more difficult to maintain chemistry balance. Combined with elevated calcium hardness (above 400 ppm) and high pH, precipitation cloudiness becomes chronic.

Key indicators:

  • Persistent cloudiness despite correct pH and chlorine
  • CH above 400 ppm
  • Scale visible on pool surfaces
  • LSI (Langelier Saturation Index) calculates strongly positive

Fix: Lower pH first. If scale and cloudiness persist despite correct pH, a partial drain (25–33%) and refill dilutes the accumulated minerals. No chemical treatment resolves truly high TDS — dilution is the only permanent fix.

Cause 4: Algae (Early Stage)

Algae does not start green. The earliest stage of an algae bloom is a slight haziness, often with a faint greenish tint that is easy to miss indoors or in a pool with colored plaster. By the time water is visibly green, algae is well-established. Catching it at the hazy stage allows for much faster treatment.

Key indicators:

  • FC below minimum effective level for the CYA level
  • Slight greenish or yellowish tint to cloudiness
  • Cloudy water worst in corners and on stairs (dead zones)
  • Slippery feel on pool walls when brushed

Fix: Full algae treatment protocol — lower pH to 7.2, shock aggressively to 40% of CYA, brush thoroughly, run filter continuously. Do not use clarifier during active algae treatment; the coagulated particles include dead algae that must be filtered out.

Cause 5: Filtration Failure

A pool with a dirty, torn, or undersized filter can have perfect chemistry and still be cloudy. The filter's job is to remove fine particles that chlorine cannot. When the filter is compromised, those particles stay in suspension.

Key indicators:

  • Chemistry tests are all in range
  • Filter pressure at or above the "backwash" threshold
  • Filter run time has been insufficient (less than 8 hours/day)
  • Cartridge filter has not been cleaned or replaced in over a season
  • DE filter media was not recharged after last backwash

Fix: Service the filter — backwash sand or DE, clean or replace cartridges. For DE filters, verify correct media charge (1 lb per 5 sq ft of filter area). Increase daily runtime to 10–12 hours until water clears.

Warning: Do not add clarifier to a pool with a compromised filter. Clarifier coagulates particles and can cause filter media to blind off completely, dropping flow to near zero. Fix the filter first, then consider clarifier if needed after the filter is confirmed working.

Cause 6: Post-Shock Particulate Cloudiness

This is a normal, temporary condition. Heavy shocking rapidly oxidizes large amounts of organic matter, creating a sudden load of fine dead-organic particles. The water turns hazy or white-cloudy within hours of a large shock dose. This is a sign the shock worked — not a sign of a problem.

Key indicators:

  • Cloudiness appeared within 6–12 hours of a heavy shock
  • Water was clear before shocking
  • No other chemistry abnormality

Fix: Run filter continuously and wait 24–48 hours. If still cloudy after 48 hours, a clarifier dose can help coagulate remaining fine particles for the filter to capture.

Using Clarifier and Flocculant: When and How

Clarifier

Clarifiers (also called coagulants) work by causing fine particles to clump together into larger particles that the filter can capture. Products like BioGuard Polysheen Plus, Hayward Polysheen, or Natural Chemistry Pool First Aid use polyquaternary ammonium compounds or chitosan-based agents.

When to use clarifier:

  • Chemistry is balanced and filter is confirmed working
  • Cloudiness is from fine particulate — the "everything is right but water is still a bit hazy" scenario
  • Post-shock cleanup when cloudiness persists beyond 48 hours

Dosing: follow label directions carefully. More clarifier is not better — overdosing causes the water to go from cloudy to milky white as excess clarifier forms its own colloidal suspension.

Flocculant

Flocculant (alum, or "floc") causes particles to coagulate so rapidly and completely that they sink to the bottom as a visible cloud of sediment. This is faster than clarifier but requires manual vacuuming to waste — the flocced material must be vacuumed out before it can cloud the water again.

When to use floc:

  • When you need water clear fast (party, inspection, etc.) and cannot wait 24–48 hours for clarifier + filter
  • After heavy rain or algae treatment where large amounts of dead material are in suspension

Important: Floc only works if you have a multiport valve on your filter set to waste — you must vacuum to waste, not to filter, or the sediment immediately returns to the water.

The Correct Treatment Sequence for Any Cloudy Pool

  1. Test all parameters and document results
  2. Identify the primary cause using the diagnosis table above
  3. Service the filter if there is any doubt about its performance
  4. Correct chemistry imbalance (pH, TA, FC) before using clarifier
  5. Shock if FC is low or CC is elevated
  6. Run filter continuously during treatment
  7. Add clarifier only after chemistry is corrected and filter is confirmed working
  8. Retest after 24 hours to confirm recovery direction

Frequently Asked Questions

How long does it take to clear cloudy pool water?
It depends on the cause. Chlorine-related cloudiness from shocking usually clears within 24–48 hours with continuous filtration. Chemistry imbalance cloudiness (high pH, high CH with high pH) can take 24–72 hours to resolve after correction. Filtration-related cloudiness may take 3–5 days if the filter was significantly underperforming. Algae-related cloudiness can take 5–10 days through a full treatment cycle.
Does clarifier actually work on cloudy pool water?
Yes, but only for cloudiness caused by fine particulate matter that is passing through the filter. Clarifier works by coagulating small particles into larger clumps that the filter can catch. It does not work on cloudiness caused by chemistry imbalance (like high pH) or active algae. Using clarifier without fixing the underlying chemistry or filtration issue means the cloudiness returns.
Can I swim in a cloudy pool?
It depends on why it is cloudy. If free chlorine is adequate (at least 1–3 ppm and appropriate for CYA level), pH is in range, and cloudiness is from a minor particulate issue, swimming is generally safe. If the pool is cloudy because chlorine is low, combined chlorine is high, or algae is present, do not swim — these conditions indicate actual sanitation failure. When in doubt, test first.
Why does my pool get cloudy after I shock it?
Post-shock cloudiness is very common and usually temporary. Shocking rapidly oxidizes organic compounds, creating a temporary surge of dead organic particles that turn the water hazy. If you shocked with calcium hypochlorite, the calcium ions can also temporarily raise water calcium and cause mild haziness. Run your filter continuously after shocking and the water should clear within 24–48 hours.
What is the difference between cloudy and milky pool water?
Milky white cloudiness is typically caused by air bubbles (from a plumbing air leak or pump issue) or very high calcium hardness precipitating in high-pH conditions. Standard cloudy or hazy water is usually from fine organic particles or chemistry imbalance. If the cloudiness is bright white and appears suddenly, check for air leaks in the pump lid and suction lines before treating chemistry.
