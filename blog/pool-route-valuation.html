How to Value a Pool Route: What Buyers Actually Pay

📅 April 15, 2026Pool Pro⏱ 6 min read

Pool routes are one of the most established small business acquisition markets in the service trades. They trade regularly, have consistent pricing conventions, and represent a proven path to business ownership without the risk of building from scratch. Whether you are buying a route to enter the industry, selling a route you have built, or adding accounts to an existing operation, understanding the valuation methodology is essential before any transaction.

The Basic Formula

Pool route valuation in the residential service market uses a monthly revenue multiple. The standard range is 10–15 times monthly gross service revenue. This is the starting point for every negotiation — the actual multiple landed depends on the quality factors described below.

Example: A route billing $8,500/month in service fees has a base valuation range of $85,000–$127,500. Where within that range the actual sale price lands depends on factors that affect buyer risk.

Factors That Push the Multiple Higher

Geographic Density

Routes where accounts are tightly clustered — ideally within a 3–5 mile radius — command premium multiples because the buyer can service more accounts per day, reducing labor cost per account. A 60-account route clustered in two neighborhoods is worth significantly more than a 60-account route spread across 15 miles of driving.

Account Tenure and Signed Contracts

Accounts with 3+ year histories are lower churn risk. Signed annual or multi-year service contracts provide contractual revenue certainty that month-to-month accounts don't. Buyers pay more for predictable revenue. A route where 80% of accounts have been with the seller for 5+ years and have signed agreements commands premium pricing.

Higher-Value Account Mix

Routes that include commercial accounts, high-end residential pools with automation, or accounts with regular equipment repair work generate more revenue per stop and have higher replacement cost than basic residential service. A route averaging $180/month per account is worth more per account than one averaging $110/month.

Clean Chemistry Documentation

Sellers who can show 12–24 months of service records and chemistry logs for each account demonstrate professional operations and reduce the buyer's risk of inheriting chemistry problems. This is where SplashLens service documentation has direct economic value — organized records show buyers exactly what they are purchasing.

The single fastest way to increase your route's sale value is to document it thoroughly. Two routes with identical account counts and identical revenue will have different sale prices if one has complete service records and the other is operating from memory. Buyers pay for certainty, and records create certainty.

Factors That Push the Multiple Lower

All-Inclusive Chemical Contracts

Chemicals-included contracts are common but reduce buyer confidence in margin predictability. If the seller has been absorbing chemical cost increases without raising prices, the buyer inherits that situation. Routes where chemicals are billed separately or where pricing has been updated recently command better multiples.

Equipment in Poor Condition

Buyers conduct equipment condition assessments during due diligence. Accounts with aging pumps, filters past service life, or automation systems that haven't been maintained reduce the multiple — the buyer is inheriting near-term repair obligations. Sellers who have maintained equipment proactively are rewarded in valuation.

High Account Turnover

If the seller has been losing 15–20% of accounts annually and replacing them with new ones, the stated revenue is accurate but the account stability is not. Buyers look at trailing 12-month churn, not just current account count. High historical churn reduces the multiple significantly.

Seller-Dependent Client Relationships

If clients have a deep personal relationship with the selling technician and there is risk of cancellation after the sale, the revenue is less transferable. This risk is higher for smaller routes where the seller is personally known to every client. Sellers who have introduced key clients to the buyer and facilitated a relationship transfer command better prices.

The Due Diligence Process

Before committing to a purchase price, buyers should:

  1. Request 12 months of bank deposits or payment processor statements to verify stated revenue
  2. Review copies of all client service agreements
  3. Ride along with the seller for 1–2 full service days, meeting clients personally
  4. Inspect equipment condition at each account (or a representative sample)
  5. Verify the seller's chemical cost records to assess actual margins
  6. Check for any pending client disputes or equipment liability claims

Seller Financing

Many pool route transactions include seller financing as a risk-sharing mechanism. The seller carries a note for 30–50% of the purchase price for 1–2 years, with payments contingent on account retention. If accounts are lost in the first year, the note balance is reduced proportionally. This structure aligns seller and buyer incentives and is particularly common when the seller's relationships with clients are the primary value driver.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the standard multiple for pool route valuation?

Residential pool service routes in most US markets sell for 10–15 times monthly gross service revenue. Routes with all-inclusive chemical contracts, high geographic density, strong multi-year client tenure, and low churn history command multiples at the top of this range. Routes with chemistry billing issues, spread-out geography, or high account turnover sell toward the bottom.

Are chemicals included in the route valuation?

Route valuation is typically based on service revenue — the base contract amount — not chemical revenue. If the route uses a chemicals-included model, the stated monthly revenue already includes chemicals and the buyer inherits that obligation. Always clarify exactly what is included in the monthly revenue figure before applying the multiple.

How do I verify a pool route before buying it?

Request 12 months of billing records and bank deposits to verify stated revenue. Ride along on the route with the seller for at least one full day. Check for signed service contracts with each account. Verify the seller's chemical cost ratio to assess actual margins. Meet key clients personally to evaluate relationship transfer risk.

How long does it take to recoup a pool route purchase?

At a 10x monthly revenue purchase price with 40% net profit margins, the payback period is approximately 25 months. At 15x monthly revenue with the same margins, payback extends to approximately 37 months. Most buyers finance pool route purchases and factor the monthly payment into the profitability model — the key metric is whether the route cash flows positively from month one after loan payments.
